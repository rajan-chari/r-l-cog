You can run every experiment, have all the data, be provably right — and still get quietly robbed.  This is an essay about truth and belief, and the gap between them.  When what you believe drifts from what is true, someone who sees that gap more clearly than you takes your money.  The obvious version is just knowing more than the other person.  The version I care about is sneakier: beliefs that pass every test you throw at them and are still, quietly, wrong.

A simple example.  Let's say you believe that a coin is a fair coin — i.e., it comes up heads 50% of the time.  Someone says, heads you have to do the dishes, tails, I will.  You accept.  But the true probability is 90% heads with this coin.

A sneakier example.  You look around and find that all the kids love yellow sneakers.  They prefer them over blue and orange sneakers.  You carefully create experiments.  You find out that given the same type of sneakers, kids are more likely to choose yellow (80%) over blue (15%) or orange (5%).  You go to the local shoe store.  They sell all shoes at the same prices.  You go and buy all the yellow shoes and when kids go look for yellow shoes and can't find them you sell them at a premium.  Now that you succeeded here, you do this same thing in the neighboring town.  But it does not work!  You dig into it and realize that the kids prefer blue sneakers.  Instead of just doing preference tests you ask why?  You realize that the school colors match the kids' preference.  The preference was real, but it was a symptom.  Some other underlying phenomenon, the school colors, sets the true distribution.  Your belief only captured the symptom, so it worked in the first town and broke in the second — i.e., truth diverges from belief and you are now punished.

Let's name what's going on.  In every example, the thing you "believe" isn't a single fact; it's a *probability distribution* over outcomes (50/50 for the coin, 80/15/5 for the sneakers).  "Belief diverges from truth" is just shorthand for your distribution not matching the real one — the distribution is where belief and truth actually live, and where they drift apart.

What do I mean by truth?  In the two examples above, it took the form of a *true distribution*: the distribution that actually generates the outcomes, whether or not anyone measures it.  The coin really does land heads 90% of the time even if you never flip it.  Running experiments does not create the truth; it is just how you estimate it.  And truths can be more general than distributions.  For example, the world is not flat.  In a second, I will show you how to take advantage of that particular truth.

What about this word belief?  Belief is someone's estimation of the true distribution.  It may or may not be true.  The more general case of course is just a statement about the world.

Let's say you come across someone who knows the world is flat.  You offer them insurance: if their ship sails off the edge of the world, you will cover the ship and its contents in full.  The premium is a low-low 10%.  To them it looks like cheap protection against a real danger.  To you it is free money, because you know the edge does not exist and the claim will never come.

All of these are the same gap underneath.  Whoever holds the belief that is less wrong gets to exploit whoever holds the belief that is more wrong.  That never changes.  The truer belief wins; it does not even have to be true, only less wrong than the one across the table.  What changes is how you ended up on the wrong side of it.  With the coin and the ship, you simply did not know, and someone who did took your money.  Information asymmetry is exploitable, everyone already believes that.  It is the boring case, because at least ignorance can feel like ignorance.

The sneakers are the same exploit with the danger hidden.  You ran the experiments.  You had the data.  You were right, until you weren't.  That gap did not come from not knowing.  It came from learning a symptom and calling it a cause.  "Yellow" was never the real variable; it was a proxy for the school colors, and it only looked like the truth because the two lined up in the first town.  Move to a town where they don't, and your belief quietly stops being true.  The exploit is identical to the coin.  The difference is that this one is invisible from the inside: it comes dressed as knowledge, well-tested, profitable, and shallow all at once.

That is what locality means: truth comes in degrees of it.  A *local truth* holds where you measured it: kids prefer yellow, in this town, this year.  A *less-local truth* holds in more places: kids prefer their school colors, whatever town you are in.  The trap is that inside its own town, a local truth is indistinguishable from a less-local one.  They give the same data and the same payoff.  The only difference shows up when you travel.

And there is no universal truth at the bottom.  The school-colors truth travels farther than yellow, but something sits beneath it too, far enough away that it breaks as well.  Every belief is local at some scale; you just haven't found the town that breaks it yet.  So the goal was never to find the truth.  It was to hold a belief that travels farther than the one you are betting against.  You do not have to be right.  You only have to be less local than the other guy.
